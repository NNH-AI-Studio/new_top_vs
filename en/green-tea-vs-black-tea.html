Frequently Asked Questions

Is green tea actually healthier than black tea?
Yes, scientifically proven. Green tea contains higher levels of EGCG (epigallocatechin gallate), the most powerful antioxidant in tea. EGCG is largely destroyed during black tea's oxidation process. Studies show green tea provides: 1) Stronger cancer prevention (breast, prostate, colorectal), 2) Better weight loss (boosts metabolism 4-5% vs. black tea's 1-2%), 3) Superior brain protection (reduces Alzheimer's risk by 50%), 4) Greater fat-burning (10-17% increase vs. minimal for black tea). However, black tea isn't unhealthy - it still contains theaflavins and thearubigins (different antioxidants formed during oxidation) that improve heart health and reduce stroke risk. Both teas lower cholesterol and blood pressure. Difference: green tea is preventative medicine; black tea is general wellness. If maximizing health benefits is priority #1, green tea wins. If you hate green tea's taste, black tea provides 60-70% of green tea's benefits while tasting better to most Western palates.
Which tea has more caffeine - green or black?
Black tea has nearly double the caffeine. Average green tea: 20-45mg caffeine per cup. Average black tea: 40-70mg per cup. For context, coffee has 95-200mg. Why the difference? Oxidation process concentrates caffeine in black tea leaves. Also, brewing method matters: black tea uses boiling water (extracts more caffeine); green tea uses cooler water (160-180¬∞F) which extracts less. Practical implications: 1) Morning wake-up = black tea (comparable to half a coffee). 2) Afternoon pick-me-up = green tea (gentle energy without jitters). 3) Evening relaxation = green tea (won't disrupt sleep). Myth: "Green tea has no caffeine." False - it has caffeine, just less. If extremely caffeine-sensitive, choose decaf versions of either tea. L-theanine in both teas (higher in green) balances caffeine's effects, creating calm alertness vs. coffee's jittery energy. Bottom line: need energy? Black tea. Want focus without overstimulation? Green tea.
Why does green tea taste bitter when I make it?
You're using water that's too hot or steeping too long. Green tea is extremely temperature-sensitive. Boiling water (212¬∞F) denatures delicate compounds, releasing bitter tannins and destroying sweet, umami flavors. Perfect green tea requires: 1) Water temperature: 160-180¬∞F (70-80¬∞C). Boil water, then let cool 3-5 minutes, or use thermometer. 2) Steep time: 1-3 minutes maximum. Set timer - even 30 seconds extra ruins it. 3) High-quality leaves: cheap tea bags use broken leaves (more surface area = more bitterness). Upgrade to whole-leaf loose tea. 4) Water quality: chlorinated tap water adds off-flavors. Use filtered water. Black tea forgives mistakes - you can use boiling water, steep 5+ minutes, and it tastes fine (maybe stronger, but not ruined). Green tea punishes errors. Pro tip: if green tea is still too bitter with correct brewing, try different varieties. Japanese Sencha and Gyokuro are sweeter than Chinese Dragon Well. Matcha (powdered) eliminates brewing errors entirely.
Can I add milk to green tea like I do with black tea?
You can, but shouldn't. Milk clashes with green tea's delicate, grassy flavors, creating unpleasant taste. British milk tea tradition developed specifically for black tea's robust, malty flavors that complement milk's creaminess. Chemical reason: milk proteins bind to green tea's catechins (the healthy antioxidants), potentially reducing their bioavailability. You're drinking green tea for health benefits - milk may neutralize them. Cultural note: in East Asia where green tea originates, adding milk is never done (considered sacrilege). If green tea tastes too bitter/bland on its own, solutions: 1) Add honey or lemon (traditional, doesn't clash), 2) Try flavored green teas (jasmine green tea is naturally sweet), 3) Cold brew green tea overnight (zero bitterness, naturally sweet), 4) Switch to black tea if you need milk. Exception: Matcha lattes work because Matcha's strong flavor stands up to milk, and it's whisked into foam (different preparation). Regular green tea + milk = disappointing experience.
Does green tea really help with weight loss?
Yes, but don't expect miracles. Green tea increases fat burning by 10-17% and boosts metabolism slightly (burn extra 75-100 calories daily). Over time this adds up: 100 calories/day √ó 365 days = 36,500 calories/year = ~10 pounds potential weight loss. However, this assumes: 1) You drink 3-5 cups daily, 2) You don't add sugar/honey (negates calorie deficit), 3) You maintain calorie deficit through diet/exercise (tea alone won't compensate for bad diet). Mechanism: EGCG + caffeine increase norepinephrine, hormone that signals fat cells to break down fat. Green tea specifically targets visceral fat (belly fat). Studies show 12 weeks of green tea extract reduces waist circumference by 2-3 inches on average. Black tea has minimal weight loss effect (lacks EGCG). Realistic expectations: green tea is supplement, not solution. Pair with calorie deficit and exercise for results. Don't expect to lose weight drinking green tea while eating pizza daily. Best use: replace sugary drinks (soda, juice) with unsweetened green tea = 200-300 calorie reduction daily + metabolic boost.
Can I drink both green tea and black tea, or should I pick one?
Drink both - they serve different purposes and there's no conflict. Ideal daily routine: 1) Morning: Black tea (higher caffeine for wake-up, pairs with breakfast). 2) Mid-morning/Afternoon: Green tea (sustained energy, health boost). 3) Evening: Green tea (low caffeine, aids digestion, relaxation). This maximizes benefits: black tea's robust flavor and energy boost when needed, green tea's health benefits throughout day. No health risks from mixing - both are safe, healthy beverages. Some people drink 5-10 cups of tea daily without issues. Concerns: 1) Caffeine total: if you're drinking 3 black teas + 2 green teas + 2 coffees = too much caffeine (anxiety, jitters, sleep disruption). Keep total under 400mg caffeine daily. 2) Iron absorption: both teas inhibit iron absorption when consumed with meals. Drink tea between meals or take iron supplement separately. 3) Cost: buying multiple tea types is expensive. Budget-conscious? Pick one based on priority (health = green tea, flavor = black tea). Many tea enthusiasts collect dozens of varieties - variety is encouraged, not problematic.

Which is more cost-effective?

Online shopping often provides better prices due to lower overhead costs and easy price comparison, while in-store shopping may offer immediate access to sales and no shipping fees.

Which offers better customer service?

In-store shopping typically provides better immediate customer service with face-to-face interaction, while online shopping offers 24/7 chat support and detailed self-service resources.

Can I return items from both?

Both offer return options, but in-store returns are generally faster and easier, while online returns may require packaging and mailing items back.

Which is better for trying on clothes?

In-store shopping is essential for clothing and shoes that need to be tried on for proper fit, while online shopping works well for standardized items with reliable sizing charts.
